# Legal PII Detection and Public-Entity Preservation for a California Research Chatbot

## Executive summary

I did **not** find a publicly documented, production-grade **English / U.S.-legal** pre-trained model that convincingly performs both halves of your task at once: **redacting private legal PII** while **preserving public legal entities** such as case citations, statutes, court names, judges acting in judicial capacity, and published scholarship. The closest English artifact is **Symio-ai/legal-pii-redactor**, whose model card explicitly says it preserves party names, attorney information, case numbers, and court information while redacting SSNs, financial accounts, minor names, and medical data; however, it provides only self-described training sources and target metrics, not independently verifiable benchmark results or a released training corpus. The strongest research-grade legal de-identification systems I found are **Korean**, especially **Thunder-DeID** and the very recent **K-LegalDeID**, which show that the task is technically feasible but do not solve your English / California deployment need directly. citeturn6view4turn6view5turn13view0turn12search0

If you want a **public dataset** you can use **without doing your own initial annotation**, the best directly relevant one is **TAB — the Text Anonymization Benchmark**: 1,268 English ECHR judgments, MIT-licensed, with labels for entity type, confidential status, co-reference, and—most importantly—**identifier_type = DIRECT / QUASI / NO_MASK**, which is the clearest public “mask vs preserve” supervision I found in legal text. For preserve-side legal entities, the best complements are **InLegalNER** (MIT; 14 legal entity types such as COURT, JUDGE, STATUTE, PRECEDENT, CASE_NUMBER) and **E-NER** (U.S. SEC legal filings; hand-annotated). I did **not** find a California-specific public dataset with the exact redact/private vs preserve/public labeling you need. citeturn17view0turn18view1turn18view2turn10view0turn22view0

The main gap in the landscape is clear: legal NLP has mostly split into **de-identification** on one side and **legal NER / citation extraction** on the other, while your product needs a **joint policy layer** that says “preserve this public legal reference, redact that private client detail.” That gap appears to exist partly because truly representative legal-confidential training data is hard to release, and partly because the problem is still underexplored rather than fundamentally blocked. citeturn16search0turn7view5turn21search2turn24search2turn24search12

## Part A pre-trained models for legal-domain PII detection

**SNU Thunder-DeID family — THUNDER Research Group.** Source: Hugging Face model cards plus GitHub inference repository. **License:** model weights are **CC BY-NC-SA 4.0**, which is a major commercial-use constraint. **Architecture / size:** DeBERTa-v2 family with three released sizes — **340M, 750M, and 1.5B** — trained for token classification with sequences up to 2,048 tokens; the 1.5B checkpoint is listed at roughly **4.86 GB** on Hugging Face. **Training data disclosed:** bilingual Korean-English pretraining corpus, then fine-tuning on a manually annotated Korean court-judgment dataset plus an entity mention list. **Entity categories:** very broad legal anonymization taxonomy, reported as **595 labels** and 27,402 labeled spans over 4,500 anonymized judgments. **Public-vs-private legal distinction:** partly, but not in the way you need for California; the model is designed to detect tokens needing de-identification in Korean court judgments, not to preserve U.S. legal references like California citations or statute mentions by an explicit public/private policy. **Benchmarks:** the 1.5B model reports **binary token-level micro F1 0.9910** and **token-level micro F1 0.8974** on the project’s internal validation split. **Known limitations:** Korean court domain only, criminal-case coverage only in the dataset description, non-commercial license, and validation is internal rather than on a broadly adopted external benchmark. **Last updated:** the model collection was updated in June 2025, with the current tree showing activity in 2026; the GitHub repo shows 63 commits and public inference tooling. citeturn13view0turn12search5turn13view2turn14search0turn14search1

**K-LegalDeID with KLUEBERT-CRF — EACL 2026 paper release.** Source: ACL Anthology paper page. **License / commercial status:** I did not locate a public code repository or license file in this pass, so commercial usability is **unverified**. **Architecture / size:** the paper describes an efficient **KLUEBERT-CRF** model for Korean court-judgment de-identification, but the public snippet I retrieved did not expose parameter counts or downloadable weights. **Training data disclosed:** the paper introduces **K-LegalDeID**, described as a large-scale benchmark dataset for Korean court judgments. **Entity categories / public-private distinction:** the abstract clearly frames the task as identifying PII in court judgments for de-identification, but the exact label schema was not visible in the retrieved snippet. **Benchmarks:** the paper positions itself as both a benchmark dataset and model contribution, but exact precision / recall / F1 values were not exposed in the retrieved preview. **Known limitations:** very fresh publication, and the public artifact trail was incomplete at the time of this search. **Last updated:** paper published in April 2026. This is important because it is one of the strongest signals that the legal-de-identification problem is active research, but it is **not yet an immediately deployable English/U.S. solution** from publicly retrievable documentation. citeturn12search0turn11search1turn12search3

**Symio-ai/legal-pii-redactor — Symio AI.** Source: Hugging Face model card. **License:** **Apache-2.0**, which is attractive for commercial prototyping. **Architecture / size:** token-classification model fine-tuned from **microsoft/deberta-v3-base**; the model card does not state an exact parameter count or RAM footprint. **Training data disclosed:** the card claims training on about **200K redacted court filings**, **100K PII-annotated legal docs**, **20K court redaction orders**, **5K FRCP 5.2 / Florida Rule 2.425 opinions**, and **500K synthetic PII documents**. **Entity categories:** SSN, TAX_ID, FINANCIAL_ACCOUNT, MINOR_NAME, DOB_MINOR, HOME_ADDRESS, PHONE, EMAIL, MEDICAL, PARTY_NAME, ATTORNEY_INFO, CASE_NUMBER, and COURT_INFO. **Public-vs-private legal distinction:** this is the **only English model I found that explicitly says** it distinguishes what “must be redacted” from what is “legally required in filings,” preserving party names, attorney information, case numbers, and court identifiers while redacting more sensitive categories. **Benchmarks:** no real benchmark numbers are reported; instead the card lists **target criteria** such as recall thresholds and false-redaction targets. **Known limitations:** the entire evidence base is self-described; no public dataset card, no paper, no external validation, and no independently reproducible benchmark surfaced. **Last updated:** the model page was active in April 2026. My confidence in it as a **research lead** is moderate; my confidence in it as a **production-grade proven solution** is low. citeturn6view0turn6view4turn6view5turn26search7

**OpenNyAI legal_docs_redaction — OpenNyAI.** Source: GitHub repository. **License:** **MIT**. **Architecture / runtime footprint:** this is an **offline Dockerized redaction pipeline**, not a single model card; it relies on spaCy-based entity detection, supports PDF/DOC/DOCX, and the README recommends at least **6 GB RAM and 4 cores**, with a roughly **7.5 GB** container image for one deployment path. **Training data disclosed:** not fully documented in the surfaced README; the pipeline masks/dummy-replaces entities detected with spaCy. **Entity categories:** masking for DATE, MONEY, PERCENT, QUANTITY, TIME; dummy replacement for ORG, PERSON, PRODUCT. **Public-vs-private legal distinction:** **no explicit preserve policy** for public legal entities. In fact, because PERSON and ORG are dummy-replaced, it risks over-redacting judges, parties, courts, or public organizations unless you add custom logic on top. **Benchmarks:** none reported in the surfaced repo snapshot. **Known limitations:** human review is explicitly required; the authors state automatic redaction can miss entities and that formatting may degrade. **Last updated:** public repo with 24 commits. This is a useful engineering baseline for **offline trust-boundary redaction**, but not a ready-made preserve-public-entities solution. citeturn27view0

**OpenNyAI en_legal_ner_trf — OpenNyAI.** Source: Hugging Face model card plus GitHub repo. **License:** the repo is **Apache-2.0**, while one metadata field on Hugging Face mentions MIT—so there is **license metadata inconsistency** and the repo license should be treated as the authoritative primary source. **Architecture / size:** spaCy transformer NER pipeline developed on top of legal-NER work for Indian judgments; the GitHub repo describes a RoBERTa-based transformer setup, though the exact parameter count is not surfaced. **Training data disclosed:** trained on **InLegalNER**, a legal-NER corpus built from Indian court judgments annotated by legal experts and data scientists. **Entity categories:** LAWYER, COURT, JUDGE, PETITIONER, RESPONDENT, CASE_NUMBER, GPE, DATE, ORG, STATUTE, WITNESS, PRECEDENT, PROVISION, OTHER_PERSON. **Public-vs-private legal distinction:** not a PII detector, but it is highly relevant for the **preserve** side of your pipeline because it identifies precisely the kinds of public legal entities you do not want a generic PII model to redact. **Benchmarks:** self-reported **F1 91.076**, precision **91.979**, recall **90.19**. **Known limitations:** Indian-judgment domain, not optimized for confidential-client PII categories, and no direct redact/preserve policy layer. **Last updated:** model and dataset pages show updates in May 2024, with repo history beyond that. In practice, this is a compelling candidate for a **preservation pre-pass**, not for end-to-end privacy filtering. citeturn9view0turn8view0

**Bottom line for Part A.** In public sources, I found **legal de-identification systems**, **legal-entity recognizers**, and **offline redaction pipelines**, but not a clearly validated English model that already solves your combined requirement at production grade. The highest-confidence conclusion is that the current best architecture for your use case is likely **hybrid**: a legal citation / public-entity preservation pass plus a configurable PII detector, rather than a single off-the-shelf legal model. citeturn13view0turn6view4turn27view0turn9view0

## Part B public datasets for legal PII annotation

**Directly relevant annotated dataset: TAB — Text Anonymization Benchmark.** Curators: Norsk Regnesentral and collaborators. Source: GitHub, ACL paper, and Hugging Face mirror. **License:** **MIT**. **Size:** **1,268** English-language ECHR judgments; the Hugging Face mirror reports **2,208 rows** because some documents have multiple annotations / annotators. **Jurisdiction / document type:** European Court of Human Rights decisions in English. **Annotation schema:** each entity mention carries `entity_type`, `identifier_type`, `confidential_status`, character offsets, and co-reference links. **Public-vs-private distinction:** **yes, and this is the key reason it matters** — `identifier_type` is explicitly **DIRECT**, **QUASI**, or **NO_MASK**. That makes TAB the clearest public legal dataset I found for learning “redact this, preserve that.” **Quality notes:** manual annotation, some multi-annotator documents, and a `quality_checked` field indicates whether another annotator revised the document. **Use in published work:** introduced in the 2022 Computational Linguistics paper and reused in later text-anonymization work. **Known issues:** ECHR is not U.S. law, so the social/legal meaning of what should remain public does not map perfectly to California practice. Even so, as supervision for a redact/preserve objective, TAB is unusually close to your need. citeturn17view0turn18view0turn18view1turn18view2turn18view3turn18view4turn16search0

**Directly relevant but non-English dataset: SNU Thunder-DeID Annotated Court Judgments.** Curator: THUNDER Research Group. Source: Hugging Face dataset card and GitHub tooling. **License:** the related public materials strongly indicate **CC BY-NC-SA 4.0** or otherwise non-commercial constraints; I would treat this dataset family as **not commercially clean until independently verified**. **Size:** **4,500** anonymized and manually annotated Korean court judgments with **27,402** labeled spans and a highly granular 595-label taxonomy. **Jurisdiction / document type:** Korean criminal court judgments. **Annotation schema:** placeholder-based inline annotations paired with an entity mention list for full supervision generation. **Public-vs-private distinction:** usable for legal de-identification, but not explicitly organized around U.S.-style preserve/public legal references. **Quality notes:** manual annotation and reviewer workflow are emphasized in project materials. **Use in published work:** Thunder-DeID paper. **Known issues:** Korean only; non-commercial constraints. This is the strongest **de-identification** dataset I found, but not the best transferable supervision for California preserve-vs-redact logic. citeturn13view0turn13view2turn14search0turn14search1

**Directly relevant new benchmark: K-LegalDeID.** Curator: authors of the EACL 2026 paper. Source: ACL Anthology paper page. **License:** not publicly verified in this pass. **Size / schema / exact categories:** not visible in the retrieved paper snippet. **Jurisdiction / document type:** Korean court judgments. **Public-vs-private distinction:** clearly de-identification-oriented; exact boundary-label design was not visible. **Quality notes / use:** fresh peer-reviewed benchmark contribution. **Known issues:** public data / code / license trail was incomplete when I searched. I include it because it is evidence that benchmarked legal de-identification datasets are appearing, but it is not yet a dependable practical starting point for your stack. citeturn11search1turn12search0

**Best common-law legal-entity dataset for the preserve side: InLegalNER.** Curator: OpenNyAI. Source: Hugging Face dataset card and model card. **License:** **MIT** on the dataset card, though access requires agreeing to share contact information. **Size:** the associated paper reports **46,545 annotated legal named entities** across **14 legal entity types**. **Jurisdiction / document type:** Indian court judgments. **Annotation schema:** legal roles and authorities, including COURT, JUDGE, PETITIONER, RESPONDENT, STATUTE, PROVISION, CASE_NUMBER, PRECEDENT, and more. **Public-vs-private distinction:** **no explicit redact/preserve label**, but it gives strong coverage of the public legal entities you want to preserve. **Quality notes:** annotated by legal experts and data scientists; the repo describes how preambles and judgment text were sampled and annotated. **Use in published work:** NLLP 2022 paper and public model releases. **Known issues:** it is not U.S. law, and the access gate could complicate automated data pipelines. citeturn10view0turn8view0turn9view0

**Best U.S. legal-entity dataset I found: E-NER.** Curator: Ting Wai Terence Au and collaborators. Source: ACL paper and GitHub repo. **License:** **CC BY-NC-SA 4.0**. **Size:** **52** SEC EDGAR legal filings, hand-annotated. **Jurisdiction / document type:** U.S. legal corporate filings from EDGAR rather than case law. **Annotation schema:** PERSON, COURT, BUSINESS, GOVERNMENT, LOCATION, LEGISLATION / ACT, and MISCELLANEOUS. **Public-vs-private distinction:** no; this is legal NER, not de-identification. **Quality notes:** hand-annotated, but small. **Use in published work:** introduced in the 2022 NLLP workshop paper, mainly to show domain shift from general English NER. **Known issues:** tiny size, filing domain rather than litigation text, and non-commercial license. Still, it is one of the few public U.S.-legal NER sets with explicit COURT and LEGISLATION labels. citeturn22view0turn21search2

**Civil-law fallback entity dataset: LeNER-Br.** Curator: University of Brasília collaborators. Source: Hugging Face and GitHub. **License:** I could not verify a clean license from the surfaced primary materials; Hugging Face marks it as **unknown**. **Size:** **66** manually annotated Brazilian legal documents. **Jurisdiction / doc type:** Brazilian legal cases and legislation. **Annotation schema:** persons, locations, time entities, organizations, legislation, and legal cases. **Public-vs-private distinction:** no explicit redact/preserve label. **Quality notes:** manual annotations; a public paper and code are available. **Use in published work:** legal NER benchmarking in Portuguese. **Known issues:** small, civil-law domain, and license uncertainty limits immediate commercial use. citeturn23search0turn23search2turn23search6

**Large U.S. legal corpora that are useful for weak supervision, silver labels, or evaluation — but not direct training targets for your policy layer.** The **Caselaw Access Project** now provides bulk U.S. case law through 2020 and licenses caselaw data and metadata under **CC0 1.0**; it includes all official state and federal published case law, with human-reviewed metadata and OCR-derived text, but **no private-vs-public annotation schema**. **CourtListener** provides bulk federal and state case law, dockets, oral arguments, judges, and even embeddings; again, excellent raw material, but not a de-identification dataset. The **Common Pile** CAP subset exposes an estimated **5.5 million** rows / **24.3 GB** and carries document-level license metadata such as “Public Domain,” which makes it attractive for building silver preserve-class labels from citations and captions. **Pile-FreeLaw** and related Free Law Project derivatives similarly provide very large raw case-law corpora but no annotation of what should be redacted. citeturn37search3turn37search1turn37search0turn35view1turn36view0turn36view1turn20search6

**Benchmarks and corpora I checked that are mostly adjacent, not directly useful for your labeling problem.** **LexGLUE** is a benchmark built from seven existing legal NLP datasets, but it is a task suite rather than a PII or legal-NER corpus for redact/preserve supervision. **LegalBench** is a broad legal reasoning benchmark, currently 162 tasks, again not a de-identification dataset. **CUAD** is contract-review clause labeling with 510 contracts and 13,000+ labels across 41 clause types, not PII. **Multi-LexSum** is a summarization dataset for U.S. civil-rights litigation. These are valuable if you later want a full legal evaluation stack, but they do not solve the privacy labeling problem. citeturn38search0turn38search1turn38search10turn38search11

**California-specific result.** I did **not** locate a public **California-specific** dataset with token- or span-level labels separating **public legal entities to preserve** from **private client information to redact**. California case law is readily available through CAP-derived corpora, CourtListener collections, and California courts’ public opinions, but the missing piece is the annotation layer. citeturn37search3turn35view1turn19search0

## Part C academic work on legal PII and legal-entity recognition

**K-LegalDeID: A Benchmark Dataset and KLUEBERT-CRF for De-identification of Korean Court Judgments** — published in **EACL 2026**. This is the clearest sign that legal de-identification is becoming a first-class benchmark problem rather than an ad hoc compliance task. Relative to your task, its main contribution is showing that court-judgment de-identification can be formalized as a benchmark + model pair, but the public English/U.S. portability and licensing situation were not yet clear in the artifacts I retrieved. citeturn11search1turn12search0

**Thunder-DeID: Accurate and Efficient De-identification Framework for Korean Court Judgments** — **Hahm, Kim, Lee, Park, Lee**, **2025**, arXiv. Thunder-DeID contributes both a legal de-identification model family and the supporting annotated datasets / inference tooling. For your problem, it is strong evidence that “legal PII masking” is solvable as a specialized NER problem, but it also highlights how jurisdiction-specific the label space and release constraints can become. citeturn13view0turn12search14

**The Text Anonymization Benchmark: A Dedicated Corpus and Evaluation Framework for Text Anonymization** — **Pilán, Lison, Øvrelid, Papadopoulou, Sánchez, Batet**, **2022**, *Computational Linguistics*. TAB’s core contribution is exactly the missing idea most legal PII work does not expose publicly: it labels not only entity types but also **whether a mention should be masked**, plus confidential attributes and co-reference. For your use case, this is the best publicly available template for teaching a model that some legal mentions are legitimate to preserve while others are risky identifiers. citeturn16search0turn17view0

**Neural Text Sanitization with Explicit Measures of Privacy Risk** — **2022**, AACL. This work is not limited to law, but it uses the TAB benchmark and frames anonymization as an optimization problem balancing privacy risk and utility preservation. Relative to your task, that is highly relevant because your actual business objective is not “remove all names,” but “preserve legal utility while reducing leakage risk.” citeturn16search18turn16search0

**Multi-Token Completion for Text Anonymization** — **2026**, EACL. This paper evaluates a supervised identifier model plus a replacement / infilling stage on TAB, pushing beyond pure masking into more fluent anonymization. For your stack, the practical lesson is that redaction and replacement are separable stages: you can detect spans conservatively at the trust boundary, then decide later whether to mask, pseudonymize, or preserve. citeturn16search12turn16search0

**Named Entity Recognition in Indian court judgments** — **Kalamkar, Agarwal, Tiwari, Gupta, Karn, Raghavan**, **2022**, NLLP workshop. This paper introduces InLegalNER and shows that legal texts require more fine-grained categories than general NER, including judges, petitioners, provisions, statutes, and precedents. Relative to your task, it is one of the strongest public arguments for splitting the problem into **public legal entity preservation** and **private-entity redaction** rather than using a generic PERSON/ORG detector. citeturn9view0turn8view0

**E-NER — An Annotated Named Entity Recognition Corpus of Legal Text** — **Au, Lampos, Cox**, **2022**, NLLP workshop. E-NER contributes a small but hand-annotated U.S.-legal NER dataset built from SEC filings and shows that general English NER degrades badly when transferred to legal text. For your problem, the lesson is straightforward: generic PII detectors and generic NER models should be treated skeptically in legal settings unless they are re-evaluated or adapted on legal text. citeturn21search2turn22view0

**Anonymity at Risk? Assessing Re-Identification Capabilities of Large Language Models in Court Decisions** — **Nyffenegger, Stürmer, Niklaus**, **2024**, Findings of NAACL. This paper focuses on re-identification risk in anonymized court rulings rather than on detection models themselves, but it is directly relevant to your threat model: even seemingly anonymized legal text may remain re-identifiable to modern models. Its contribution to your project is to justify conservative span detection and strong evaluation against re-identification, not just token-level F1. citeturn24search12turn24search9

**eyecite: A Tool for Parsing Legal Citations** — **Cushman, Dahl, Lissner**, **2021**, *Journal of Open Source Software*. eyecite is not an anonymization paper, but it is arguably the most important preservation-side citation paper in this landscape because it makes case, short-form, statutory, journal, *supra*, and *id.* references machine-detectable at scale. Relative to your use case, it suggests a deterministic strategy: protect public citation spans before any statistical PII detector ever sees the text. citeturn32view3turn29search11

**Named Entity Recognition in Historic Legal Text: A Transformer and State Machine Ensemble Method** — **Trias, Wang, Jaume, Idreos**, **2021**, NLLP workshop. This paper addresses NER in OCR-noisy legal text, which matters because court PDFs and appended exhibits are often messy. For your product, it supports a practical point: if your ingest includes scanned filings, you will probably need OCR-aware citation and entity extraction, not just clean-text models. citeturn24search6

## Recommended action list

- **If you want to spend the least effort, do this:** build a **hybrid local sanitizer** with **eyecite** to lock / preserve citations first, a **custom public-entity allowlist** for courts / judges / statutes / public officials, and then a generic configurable detector such as **Presidio** for the redact step. That minimizes annotation burden while avoiding the worst failure mode of generic PII models: deleting legally meaningful public references. citeturn32view3turn31view0turn40search1turn40search9

- **If you want the highest quality, do this:** create a small **California-specific annotation set** and fine-tune an English detector on a blended corpus: **TAB** for redact-vs-preserve supervision, plus **InLegalNER** / **E-NER** for preserve-side legal entities, plus your own California opinions / briefs / client-confidential documents for domain adaptation. That is the shortest path to a model that actually reflects California court practice. citeturn17view0turn18view1turn10view0turn22view0turn37search3

- **The single best model to start from is:** **Symio-ai/legal-pii-redactor** if you insist on a public, English, legal-specific starting checkpoint with an explicit preserve-vs-redact policy. - **The single best dataset to start from is:** **TAB**.
