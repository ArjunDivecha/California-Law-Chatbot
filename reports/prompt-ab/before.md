## SECTION: new_requirement_impact

The instruction converts Article IV from a single-tier residuary clause into a **two-tier waterfall**: (1) a fractional charitable gift of 10% of the residue, then (2) equal division of the remaining 90% among then-living children. Because the gift is expressed as a *percentage of the residue* rather than a pecuniary amount, no abatement/funding-order machinery changes are needed — the children's shares self-adjust.

On the mechanics of making the change: unless the trust instrument provides otherwise, a revocable trust may be modified by the settlor using the procedure for revocation — [Probate Code § 15402](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=PROB&sectionNum=15402) (verified against leginfo: "Unless the trust instrument provides otherwise, if a trust is revocable by the settlor, the settlor may modify the trust by the procedure for revocation."). The packet does not include the trust's own amendment/revocation clause, so the draft below is styled as a formal amendment with a signature block; **confirm the instrument's amendment formalities before execution.**

Ancillary note: because a charity is now a trust beneficiary, on the Settlor's death the trustee's Probate Code § 16061.7 notice obligations will extend to the charity, and gifts to charities can implicate Attorney General notice in later administration — administration-stage issues, not drafting blockers.

## SECTION: drafting_strategy

1. **Full restatement of Article IV** rather than an interlineated edit — cleanest for a short article; avoids ambiguity about what survives.
2. **Percentage-of-residue structure** (10% / 90%) matching the memo's sequencing: charity first, then equal division.
3. **Qualification condition + savings clause:** condition the gift on the organization being described in § 501(c)(3) *at the time of distribution*, with trustee authority to select a substitute food bank with the same exempt purpose if the named organization no longer qualifies or has ceased to exist. This protects the charitable intent and any estate-tax charitable deduction.
4. **Placeholder for the charity's legal name, city, and EIN** — the memo does not identify the food bank; an EIN reference is best practice for unambiguous identification.
5. **Preserve the existing beneficiary language verbatim** ("then-living children," "equal shares") per the preservation directive — the pre-existing lack of a per-stirpes substitute gift is *flagged*, not silently changed, because the instruction did not authorize altering the class-gift structure.

## SECTION: review_flags

1. **Charity not identified.** The memo says only "a qualified 501(c)(3) food bank." Obtain the organization's exact legal name, city, and EIN from the client before execution — many food banks operate under DBAs (e.g., regional affiliates of national networks).
2. **Savings/substitute-charity clause is a drafter addition** not requested in the memo. It is standard protective drafting, but confirm the client is comfortable with trustee discretion to redirect the gift if the named charity fails; the client may prefer the gift to lapse to the children instead.
3. **Pre-existing gap carried forward:** "then-living children" in equal shares means a predeceased child's descendants take **nothing** and that child's share redistributes to surviving children. This may or may not match client intent — the instruction said to preserve the equal division, so I did not change it, but recommend confirming whether the client wants a right-of-representation substitute gift (cf. the default rules in Prob. Code Part 1 of Division 11, e.g., § 21102 on transferor intent controlling).
4. **Amendment formalities unverified.** The packet excerpt does not include the trust's amendment/revocation clause. Prob. Code § 15402's default (modify by the revocation procedure) yields if the instrument provides its own exclusive method — check for delivery-to-trustee, acknowledgment, or writing requirements.
5. **"Residue" computation point.** The 10% is drafted as a fraction of the residue (after debts, expenses, taxes, and any specific gifts elsewhere in the instrument). If the client intends 10% of the *gross* trust estate, the clause must be restructured — confirm.
6. **Tax note (non-blocking).** The qualification condition referencing IRC §§ 170/2055/2522 preserves charitable-deduction eligibility if the estate is taxable; no charitable remainder trust mechanics are needed because this is an outright fractional gift at death.
